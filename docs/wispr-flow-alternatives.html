The Best Open-Source Wispr Flow Alternatives for Windows (2026)

A practical, honest comparison of free and offline voice-to-text apps · Updated 2026

Wispr Flow made voice-to-text feel effortless: hold a key, talk, and clean text appears wherever you're typing. But it's a paid, closed-source, cloud service — your microphone audio leaves your machine, and you need an account and a subscription. If you'd rather have something free, open source, and 100% offline, this guide ranks the best alternatives for Windows and explains the trade-offs honestly.

What to look for in a Wispr Flow alternative: does it run locally (privacy), is it actually free, is the source open, how accurate is it, does it type into any app, and does it support your languages? Here's how the leading options stack up.

1. Sotto Best free & open-source pick

Sotto is the closest free, open-source match to the Wispr Flow experience on Windows. You hold a hotkey, speak, and accurate text is typed at your cursor in any app — terminals, Office, browsers, chat, IDEs, even elevated windows where clipboard paste is blocked. It runs OpenAI's Whisper locally on your CPU (via OpenVINO), so audio never leaves your machine. There's no account, no telemetry, no subscription — it's MIT-licensed.

Strengths: genuinely free and open source, fully offline, live "type as you speak" mode, a voice-activity gate that ignores background noise, and out-of-the-box English + Hindi/Hinglish with one-click switching (plus ~99 Whisper languages).

Trade-offs: Windows-only today, and it's a lightweight indie project rather than a polished commercial product. The app is unsigned, so SmartScreen shows a warning the first time.

2. Talon Voice

Talon is a powerful voice-control and dictation tool loved by programmers and people with RSI. It goes far beyond dictation — full hands-free computer control, custom grammars, eye tracking. It runs offline. The catch: the best speech engine (Conformer) is a paid patron beta, the learning curve is steep, and it isn't open source. If you want scriptable voice control more than quick dictation, Talon is superb; if you just want Wispr-style typing, it's heavier than you need.

3. Windows Voice Access (built in)

Windows 11 ships with Voice Access, a free, offline, built-in dictation and control feature. It's a reasonable zero-install option. But it's closed source, its accuracy and multilingual support (especially Hindi/Hinglish) lag Whisper-based tools, and it's tied to the OS. Good as a fallback; not as accurate or flexible as a Whisper app.

4. Whisper-based open-source apps (WhisperWriter, Buzz, Vibe, Whispering)

A family of open-source projects wrap OpenAI Whisper for desktop dictation or transcription: WhisperWriter and Whispering for hotkey dictation, Buzz and Vibe for transcribing audio/video files. They're free and private, and great if you like tinkering. Compared to Sotto they're often more setup-heavy, more focused on file transcription than fast type-anywhere dictation, or less tuned for CPU-only, low-latency use and mixed-language (Hindi/Hinglish) input.

5. Dragon (Nuance)

Dragon Professional is the long-standing commercial gold standard for accuracy and offline dictation. But it's expensive (one-time cost in the hundreds), closed source, and has no meaningful Hindi/Hinglish support. It's overkill unless you're doing heavy professional dictation and can pay for it.

How to choose

  • Want free, private, open source, and Wispr-like? → Sotto.
  • Want full hands-free computer control / coding by voice? → Talon.
  • Want zero install, don't mind closed source? → Windows Voice Access.
  • Transcribing long recordings/files? → Buzz/Vibe (or Sotto's upcoming unlimited full-file transcription — join the waitlist).
  • Need best-in-class commercial accuracy and can pay? → Dragon.
